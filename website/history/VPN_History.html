History of VPNs

1970s: Creation of the internet

Spawned from ARPA (the Pentagon's Advanced Research Facility) in the late 1960's early 70s, ARPANET was developed off a network system based on Packet Switching. ARPANET soon expanded fairly rapidly to incorporate research and education centers. However, ARPANET was restricted in its capabilities, since the network ran on Network Control Protocol. This allowed devices to connect ARPANET only if the devices were on the same network. There was also another model of networking that went into the development of ARPANET's development, the OSI model.

1980s: Development of the TCP/IP model

Development of the Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol model in the 1980s began the shift towards development of inter-networked remote devices. This newly developed model was based off the OSI model, but it centralized the purpose of each model layer. These layers are the Physical Layer, IP layer, Transport Layer, and Application Layer. VPNs are applications that primarily work with the IP layer, helping to encrypt remote user communications with a central server.

1985: Launch of first major online platform; AOL

Soon after the development of the TCP/IP model, AOL was established as one of the first major online platforms.

1990s: Development of early VPN protocols

PPTP

PPTP (Point-to-Point Tunneling Protocol) serves the purpose of establishing secure VPN connections over the internet. PPTP establishes these connections by encapsulating (PPP) frames in IP packets whilst they're traversing the internet. These connections however are nothing special. The VPN tunnels are created through what's called a Generic Routing Encapsulation tunnel. The tunnelling operations can be set in two different modes, voluntary and compulsory. Voluntary tunneling occurs when a client initiates a connection and the network connection is made. Compulsory tunneling occurs when the server initiates tunneling at the ISP level.

L2F

Developed by Cisco, the Layer 2 Forwarding is utilized to establish secure end point tunnels across public infrastructure connecting ISP's to enterprise home gateways.

L2TP

Unlike other protocols, LT2P (Layer 2 Tunneling Protocol) establishes connections between clients and VPN servers without encrypting client/server payloads. With this in mind, LT2P utilizes IPsec to provide encryption. IPSec also controls the data travelling between VPN endpoints. Going into more detail, once tunnels between endpoints are created, the following data transfer process ensues. Data that client wants to send is broken up into packets and then IPSec encrypts payload of the packet(s). LT2P encapsulates encrypted packets into its own packets in order to create tunnel to safely transport data through the public environment. The packets are then sent over the internet and LT2P un-encapsulates the packets at the receiving end and IPSec proceeds to decrypt them. The decrypted packets then have their original data restored enabling access to them on the LAN of the VPN server.

VPN protocols of the 2000s - 2020s

OpenVPN

OpenVPN was developed in 2001 as a reliable and secure VPN protocol. The protocol operates on two styles of transport protocols, TCP to ensure control over data transmission and UDP for faster connections. This variance enables OpenVPN to cater to wide ranges of user preferences and use cases. The backbone of security, authentication, and encryption within the protocol falls upon the OpenSSL library. OpenVPN can also serve as a VPN software. Any user will have to download VPN client in order to connect to the vpn server.

SSL VPN

Developed as a secure response to the growing need for work from home situations, the SSL VPN protocol enables users to access an organization's network without needing specialized software. Within the protocol there are two types of SSL VPNs, SSL portal VPN and SSL tunnel VPN. SSL portal VPNs operate by allowing a user a single VPN connection after entering credentials to initiate a secure connection. SSL tunnel VPNs, on the other hand, enable users to access the VPN through a browser that tunnels user into their organization through proprietary software or networks

SSTP

SSTP (Secure Socket Tunneling Protocol) operates through creating a tunnel between client devices and vpn server. This protocol was primarily developed by Microsoft to replace PPTP and L2TP in order to secure remote access to private networks over the internet. How does SSTP work though? SSTP establishes encrypted tunnels between client and server, however unlike PPTP, SSTP utilizes SSL/TLS and port 443 to manage security nad firewall traversal.
